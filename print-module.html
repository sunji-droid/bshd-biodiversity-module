Section 02

Foreword

The Imperative for Biodiversity Journalism in Botswana: Botswana holds some of the world’s most iconic and ecologically delicate natural landscapes. From the pristine wetlands of the Okavango Delta to the vast expanses of the Kalahari Desert and the rich riverine corridors of the Chobe, our nation’s biodiversity is integral not only to global conservation heritage but also to our national economy, cultural identity, and the everyday sustenance of our communities.

Yet, as we enter an era defined by intensifying climatic volatility, systemic land degradation, and resource overexploitation, these critical ecosystems face unprecedented threats. The drying of historic river systems, encroaching desertification, localized flooding events, and the escalation of human-wildlife conflicts are tangible realities affecting thousands of Batswana every day.

The Botswana Society for Human Development (BSHD), in proud partnership with ClimateFeatures and with generous support from the Internews Network, has developed this comprehensive training module specifically to equip journalists with evidence-based research methods, rights-based frameworks, investigative financial tools, and solutions-oriented reporting strategies.

Curriculum Unit 1

Unit 1: Overview of Global Biodiversity Status

1.1 Global biodiversity status

According to comprehensive assessments published by the Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services (IPBES), the Earth is undergoing the "Sixth Mass Extinction." Across the globe, approximately one million animal and plant species face extinction driven by five direct drivers: changes in land and sea use, direct exploitation of organisms, climate change, pollution, and invasive alien species.

1.2 Global biodiversity agreements and frameworks

The international community governs biodiversity protection through the Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD) and the landmark Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF). The GBF establishes 23 action-oriented targets to achieve by 2030, notably Target 3 (the 30x30 target to conserve 30% of land and sea), Target 2 (restoring 30% of degraded ecosystems), Target 15 (mandatory corporate biodiversity disclosures), and Targets 22/23 (equitable participation of Indigenous Peoples and Local Communities).

Curriculum Unit 2

Unit 2: Biodiversity and Environmental Issues in Botswana

2.1 Highlighting Key Biodiversity Areas and their Risks

Botswana's Key Biodiversity Areas (KBAs) include the Okavango Delta (vulnerable to upstream water diversion and rainfall shifts), Makgadikgadi Pans and Boteti River (threatened by mining water abstractions and fences), Chobe River wetlands (facing high seasonal elephant concentrations), and the Central Kalahari Game Reserve (subject to rangeland degradation and borehole overreliance).

2.2 Domestication of international biodiversity instruments

International conventions (`CBD`, `CITES`, `UNCCD`) are domesticated into Botswana laws through the National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan (NBSAP) and Vision 2036, which mandate decoupling economic growth from natural capital depletion.

2.3 Botswana National Ecosystem Assessment (BUAN)

Led by Prof. Wame Hambira, Prof. Masamba, and Prof. Moalafhi, the BUAN National Ecosystem Assessment provides critical empirical data showing that rangelands and wetlands contribute billions of Pula to household economies, while highlighting that over 40% of communal grazing lands face bush encroachment.

2.4 Department of Wildlife and National Parks (DWNP) & 2.5 Department of Forestry and Range Resources (DFRR)

DWNP manages protected national parks, enforces anti-poaching alongside the BDF, oversees Community-Based Natural Resource Management (CBNRM), and mitigates human-wildlife conflict. DFRR regulates forest reserves, manages rangeland rehabilitation and national firebreaks, and controls the commercial harvesting of veld products such as mophane caterpillars (*phane*) and sengaparile.

Curriculum Unit 3

Unit 3: Biodiversity, Climate Change and Sustainable Development

Climate change and biodiversity loss operate as a bi-directional feedback loop across Southern Africa. Droughts reduce surface water in the Okavango and Kalahari, forcing wildlife into agricultural corridors. Conversely, clearing dryland woodlands or overgrazing strips natural carbon sinks and moisture-retaining soil layers, prolonging local heatwaves. Journalists must frame ecological restoration through the lens of Sustainable Development Goals—specifically SDG 15 (Life on Land) and SDG 13 (Climate Action)—by highlighting Ecosystem-based Adaptation (EbA) and Nature-based Solutions (such as riparian zone revegetation and rotational grazing).

Curriculum Unit 4

Unit 4: Reporting Biodiversity Loss and Environmental Degradation

"Finding and Evaluating Environmental Story Ideas: This unit equips journalists to approach biodiversity reporting beyond its traditional framing as an environmental issue. It explores biodiversity through the lenses of rights and justice, finance and business, investigative and accountability reporting, the intertwined perspectives of biodiversity and climate change, the Sustainable Development Goals, and solutions journalism... Drawing on the global state of biodiversity, Botswana's biodiversity context, and the international conventions, national policies, and legal frameworks that govern biodiversity, this unit highlights the wealth of opportunities for story development and stronger reporting."

4.1 Tips for Finding Story Ideas: Track indicator species, audit public Environmental Impact Assessments (EIAs), and consult indigenous San and rural herder knowledge.
4.2 Evidence-Driven Reporting: Verify environmental claims using peer-reviewed literature (UB/ORI/BUAN), satellite platforms (Global Forest Watch), and official statistics (Statistics Botswana).
4.3 Rights-Based Lens: Frame stories around San community rights, prior informed consent, environmental justice, and gender-differentiated impacts.
4.4 Finance & Business Approach: Audit corporate ESG disclosures on the Botswana Stock Exchange, track TNFD nature-risk disclosures, and monitor conservation trust funds.
4.5 Follow the Money (Investigative): Investigate illicit wildlife trafficking syndicates, unlicensed river sand mining contracts, and CBNRM community revenue audits.
4.6 Solutions Journalism Framework: Apply the four pillars (Response, Evidence, Limitations, Insights) to community conservation initiatives.
4.7 Resource-Driven Global Economy (Credited to Prof. Fadhel Kabou): Examine monetary sovereignty, structural foreign debt, primary commodity export pressures, and the ecological debt owed by industrialized nations.
4.8 Journalism Ethics: Never publish exact GPS coordinates of endangered species, protect community whistleblowers, and maintain editorial independence during media junkets.
4.9 AI in Biodiversity Reporting: Leverage satellite AI and acoustic sensors for data journalism while strictly verifying AI-summarized reports against primary documents.

Curriculum Unit 5

Unit 5: The Story Clinic: What Worked, What Was Missing, and What Next

"Biodiversity journalism plays a critical role in informing the public about environmental issues and conservation challenges. Across Botswana, journalists continue to report diligently on wildlife crime, human wildlife interactions, climate impacts, habitat degradation, and conservation initiatives... However, biodiversity conservation often requires more than reporting events as they occur. While breaking news effectively answers questions such as what happened, where, and when, conservation outcomes are strengthened when reporting also explores why the issue occurred, what its implications are, and what responses may address it."

Case Study Summaries & Lessons

Case 1 (Elephant Conflict in Chobe Enclave): Moving from a breaking report on crop damage (`Kavimba village`) to an explanatory analysis of multi-year rainfall deficits, an investigative audit of Chobe Enclave Conservation Trust (`CECT`) revenues, and a solutions evaluation of chili-pepper bio-fences (`80% crop damage reduction`).

Case 2 (Sand Mining along Metsimotlhabe River): Moving from police impounding trucks (`P5,000 fines`) to an explanatory breakdown of riverbed aquifer stripping, an investigative tracking of state road construction sand tenders, and a solutions look at manufactured crushed stone sand (`M-sand`).

Case 3 (Commercial Mophane Worm Harvest): Moving from a routine DFRR seasonal permit notice (`P10 kgotla permit`) to an explanatory examination of heatwaves disrupting caterpillar pupation, an investigative probe into export markups (`600% markup by middlemen`), and a solutions profile of semi-domesticated breeding enclosures in Lerala (`140% income boost`).
