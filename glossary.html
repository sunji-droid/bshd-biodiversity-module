Key Biodiversity Reporting Terms

A Searchable Plain-Language Glossary for Environmental Journalists in Botswana.

Biodiversity Loss

Core Ecological Concept

The decline or disappearance of biological diversity within a given ecosystem, geographic area, or the planet as a whole. In reporting context across Botswana, biodiversity loss refers not only to the extinction of individual wildlife species (such as cheetahs or wattled cranes), but also the degradation of genetic diversity within populations and the structural collapse of entire habitats like the Okavango Delta or Makgadikgadi grasslands. Driven primarily by climatic shifts, pollution, habitat fragmentation, and overexploitation.

Desertification

Land Degradation & Climate

The persistent degradation of dryland ecosystems by variations in climate and human activities, leading to the expansion of arid, desert-like conditions and the loss of fertile topsoil. In Botswana, where over 70% of the landmass is covered by Kalahari sands, desertification is accelerated by prolonged multi-year droughts, localized overgrazing around boreholes, and deforestation for firewood, drastically impairing agricultural productivity and rangeland biodiversity.

Ecosystem-based Adaptation (EbA)

Solutions & Resilience

The use of biodiversity and ecosystem services as part of an overall adaptation strategy to help human communities adapt to the adverse impacts of climate change. For journalists reporting on solutions, EbA includes practices such as restoring natural wetlands to buffer against flash floods, conserving indigenous drought-resistant tree species to prevent soil erosion, and protecting natural riparian zones along the Chobe River to maintain clean water filtration during dry periods.

Ecosystem Services

Resource Economics & Rights

The direct and indirect benefits that human beings and rural communities freely obtain from properly functioning natural ecosystems. These are categorized into four distinct pillars: Provisioning services (clean drinking water, wild fruit, thatch grass, timber, fish); Regulating services (carbon sequestration, flood control, water purification, pollination of crops); Supporting services (soil formation, nutrient cycling); and Cultural services (eco-tourism revenue, spiritual heritage, aesthetic inspiration).

Habitat Fragmentation

Landscape Ecology & Wildlife

The division of large, continuous expanses of natural habitat into smaller, isolated patches separated by human infrastructure such as veterinary fences, highways, agricultural expansions, mining concessions, or urban settlements. In Botswana, habitat fragmentation directly disrupts ancient wildlife migration corridors for elephants, zebras, and wildebeests, cutting populations off from seasonal water sources and concentrating herds in small zones where overgrazing and human-wildlife conflict intensify.

Human-Wildlife Conflict (HWC)

Community Governance & Beat

Any interaction between wild animals and human populations that results in negative impacts on human lives, livelihoods, property, or wildlife survival. In Botswana, HWC commonly manifests as elephants destroying rural maize and sorghum fields, lions or wild dogs depredating livestock, or dangerous encounters near natural water sources during severe droughts. Effective journalism on HWC moves beyond sensationalizing attacks to investigate mitigation strategies, compensation mechanisms, and Community-Based Natural Resource Management (CBNRM).

Key Biodiversity Areas (KBAs)

Global Science Framework

Sites contributing significantly to the global persistence of biodiversity, identified using standardized global criteria established by the IUCN. In Botswana, designated KBAs include the Okavango Delta, the Makgadikgadi Pans, the Chobe River system, the Central Kalahari Game Reserve, and the Tswapong Hills. Monitoring KBAs provides journalists with an objective, scientific benchmark to measure whether protected areas are effectively safeguarding vulnerable habitats or experiencing degradation.

Land Degradation Neutrality (LDN)

International Policy Target

A state whereby the amount and quality of land resources necessary to support ecosystem functions and services and enhance food security remains stable or increases within specified temporal and spatial scales. Embedded in Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) Target 15.3, LDN is a critical framework for Botswana journalists evaluating government land management policies and combating the loss of fertile rangelands across communal and commercial cattle ranching areas.

Overexploitation

Resource Extraction & Accountability

The harvesting of renewable natural resources at a rate faster than they can naturally regenerate. In environmental and biodiversity reporting, overexploitation encompasses illegal wildlife poaching, unsustainable commercial or subsistence fishing in freshwater systems, excessive sand mining from riverbeds that causes bank erosion and water table drops, and the unregulated commercial harvesting of indigenous veld products such as mophane worm (phane) or devil’s claw (sengaparile).

Solutions Journalism

Reporting Practice & Method

Rigorous, evidence-based reporting on responses to social and environmental problems, rather than covering only the degradation or disaster itself. In biodiversity reporting, a Solutions Journalism approach investigates not only *that* a river is drying up or elephant conflict is increasing, but deeply evaluates a specific community or institutional response (e.g., solar-powered wildlife boreholes, chili-pepper deterrent fences, or regenerative grazing). It examines four pillars: the **Response**, the **Evidence** of effectiveness, the **Limitations**, and the replicable **Insights**.
